Our Specialized Future Planning Services

Enduring Power of Attorney (EPA)

Drafting a robust legal framework that only comes into effect if you lose the mental capacity to make decisions in the future, covering both your finances and personal welfare.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens if I lose capacity without an Enduring Power of Attorney in place?

If you lose capacity without an EPA, your family cannot automatically access your accounts or make decisions for you. They will need to apply to the Circuit Court to have a Decision-Making Representative appointed.

This process is slow, emotionally taxing, and can cost thousands of euros in legal and court fees.

Can I appoint more than one attorney?

Yes. You can appoint multiple attorneys to act “jointly” (meaning they must agree on every decision) or “jointly and severally” (meaning they can make decisions independently of one another).

We will provide candid advice on which structure best suits your family dynamic to avoid future deadlocks.

Does an Ordinary Power of Attorney cover me if I develop dementia or have a severe stroke?

No. An Ordinary Power of Attorney is immediately revoked the moment you lose mental capacity.

To ensure someone can manage your affairs if you become incapacitated, you must specifically register an Enduring Power of Attorney (EPA).
